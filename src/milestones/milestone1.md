## Project Overview
ClassManager is a system to manage the distribution of schedules for teachers, classrooms, and students.


### Problem

At the Universidade de Aveiro, as in many other institutions, this reality is well known.

The current system has critical flaws: it relies on manual processes and on knowledge concentrated in a few individuals, ignores the preferences of students and lecturers, fails to adapt to unexpected changes, and does not communicate updates in a timely manner.

The impact is clear: scattered schedules, split days, lack of time for studying, internships, or simply living.

And lecturers who spend hours solving logistical problems instead of focusing on teaching.

### Context

ClassManager emerges as the solution we all deserve.

An intelligent system that optimizes schedules to make them balanced, respecting the real needs of students and lecturers, improving room usage, and ensuring that the entire community is informed in real time whenever changes occur.

To address these challenges, the first step was understanding who would use the system and why. We identified four main personas: Secretary, Administrator, Lecturer, and Student. Each has distinct needs, yet all are interconnected by the same goal: ensuring that classes run smoothly.

The Secretary manages day-to-day operations: room bookings, teaching workload allocation, and timetable organization. The Administrator oversees everything, defines permissions, and resolves issues. The Lecturer validates schedules, requests adjustments when necessary, and books or requests room changes. And the Student simply wants to stay informed and not miss classes.

With these personas defined, we identified three key areas where we wanted to create value — our epics: Room Management, Manual Timetable Creation, and the Optimization Algorithm.

However, we decided to start with the MVP: Room Management. Because without effective control over rooms, nothing works.

Within the Room Management epic, we focused on three essential requirements:

First, the Lecturer can reserve a set of rooms for exams or assessments, submitting requests integrated with the academic timetable.

Second, the Secretary approves or rejects those requests, with full visibility over availability and conflict prevention.

Third, any user has access to real-time updated information about each room.

The other two epics focus on teaching workload distribution through timetable creation, either manually or automatically generated by the optimization algorithm.

In this way, we were able to clearly assess our problem and define a concrete, scalable solution.

## State of art

After defining the requirements we wanted to meet, we decided to analyze how timetables are currently created at Departamento de Eletrónica, Telecomunicações e Informática (DETI).

At present, the implemented solution relies on a single professor who is responsible for associating classes, lecturers, schedules, and rooms.

As you can imagine, managing this amount of information is already complex for a single degree program — let alone for all DETI programs.

The biggest problem is that the entire system depends on a single professor. When that professor eventually leaves the university, no one will be able to generate timetables, because only he understands how the algorithm works.


Given this situation, it became clear that continuing with the current solution was not viable. We went to the market in search of alternatives.

## Risks and calendar

As with any software project of some complexity, ClassManager also presents several significant risks.

One of the main risks is data consistency and the accidental overlap of classes. Since the system will integrate information from multiple departments and schedules, small errors may lead to conflicts or duplications, affecting the overall results.

Another high-level risk relates to the complexity of timetable optimization. Ensuring that it satisfies all constraints — rooms, lecturers, time slots, and groups — without generating overlaps is a considerable technical challenge.

Regarding the project timeline, future development will be organized into modules. These modules represent both the previously discussed epics and the deployment and user feedback phases.

Providing a quick overview of the schedule, we intend to begin immediately with the development of the room management module. Our goal is to complete this component by the first week of December, and then proceed to the user feedback phase and production release. This way, we will have an MVP developed, tested, and running in production at DETI, as shown in the project calendar below.
